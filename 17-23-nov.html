Weekly Market Recap — 17–23 November 2025

Published: Nov 24, 2025 • Reading time: ~10 min

This third weekly recap covers the period from 17 to 23 November 2025. Unlike the previous week, where markets mainly reacted to the political shock of the U.S. shutdown ending, this week was dominated by valuation concerns, volatility, and renewed focus on macro momentum.

Financial markets showed signs of tension: investors questioned the sustainability of high valuations in some sectors, uncertainty around monetary policy persisted, and safe-haven assets gained traction. For finance students, this is a perfect week to study how sentiment, valuation, risk perception, and macro expectations interact.

  • market volatility rises again,
  • investors reassess AI and tech valuations,
  • gold strengthens as uncertainty grows,
  • macro data slowly return after shutdown disruption.

1. Macro Environment: Back to Data, But Confidence Still Fragile

With the government reopened, attention gradually shifted back toward economic fundamentals. Markets began preparing for the return of important delayed data releases, including labour and inflation figures.

1.1 Growth and confidence

  • Investors recognised that although the shutdown was over, its economic consequences may still appear with delay.
  • Growth expectations remained positive but not strong enough to eliminate recession concerns.
  • Confidence indicators continued reflecting caution from both firms and consumers.

In other words, the shutdown resolution removed a tail risk, but did not magically restore economic momentum. Markets shifted from “political fear” to “economic doubt”.

2. Monetary Policy: Expectations Stabilise, Debate Continues

Central banks remained a dominant narrative. The main question was not whether policy was easing, but how fast and how far easing would eventually go.

2.1 Market view

  • investors still expected future rate cuts,
  • but confidence in aggressive easing remained limited,
  • communication from policy makers was cautious and conditional on data.

For valuation logic, this meant that discount rates were not expected to spike higher, but neither were they expected to collapse rapidly. Markets remained in a “controlled easing” mindset.

3. Equities: Choppiness, Rotation, and AI Under Scrutiny

The equity market narrative this week revolved around volatility and a reassessment of previous winners. Index moves alone do not tell the story; the real interest lies in how investors repositioned.

3.1 Index dynamics

  • U.S. equities experienced swings rather than clean trends,
  • global indices behaved unevenly across regions,
  • investors adopted a more selective stance.

3.2 Tech and AI concerns

One of the defining elements of this week was greater cautiousness toward high-valuation segments, particularly AI-related technology names.

  • valuation multiples were critically examined,
  • earnings expectations were questioned,
  • positioning became less complacent.

This is a useful lesson: when an investment theme drives too much of the market for too long, investors eventually reassess whether fundamentals justify prices.

4. Safe Havens & Cross-Asset Signals

While equities struggled to find clear direction, cross-asset markets sent important signals about risk appetite.

4.1 Gold strengthens

Gold continued to gain traction, reflecting investor preference for protection against uncertainty.

  • hedging demand increased,
  • risk-off positioning grew at times,
  • concerns around policy, valuations, and growth fed safe-haven interest.

4.2 Bonds and FX

  • yields fluctuated as markets adjusted rate expectations,
  • the U.S. dollar moved with changing risk appetite and policy sentiment,
  • rate differentials continued to shape currency trends.

Cross-asset consistency matters: when equities wobble, gold strengthens, yields move, and FX reacts, you are watching the same story expressed through different instruments.

5. Global Markets: Not All Regions React the Same

Global equity performance remained uneven. Regional fundamentals, currency moves, and sector composition dictated relative performance.

  • some markets benefited from weaker currencies and better export prospects,
  • others lagged due to local economic challenges,
  • emerging markets reacted to both global risk tone and domestic stories.

For global allocation, this reinforces the idea that one global narrative rarely produces one global outcome.

6. Lessons for Finance Students

  • Valuation matters — when prices stretch too far ahead of fundamentals, even small doubts can trigger volatility.
  • Macro uncertainty does not disappear instantly — removing political risk does not equal economic stability.
  • Cross-asset reading is essential — bonds, gold, FX, and equities complement each other in explaining sentiment.
  • Market narratives evolve — AI enthusiasm meets valuation discipline is a perfect example of changing investor psychology.
  • Weekly context matters — one week rarely defines the year, but it reveals how investors think.

Overall, 17–23 November 2025 can be seen as a transition week: markets moved from political relief toward renewed economic questioning, while risk appetite became more selective and disciplined.

Key takeaways (17–23 November 2025):
  • Volatility increased as investors reassessed previous optimism.
  • AI and tech valuations experienced renewed scrutiny.
  • Gold strengthened as uncertainty persisted.
  • Macro focus shifted back to data after shutdown effects.
  • Market tone turned selectively cautious rather than aggressively bearish.
