Weekly Market Recap — 24–30 November 2025

Published: Dec 1, 2025 • Reading time: ~10 min

This fourth weekly recap covers the period from 24 to 30 November 2025, a week shaped by three elements: the return of delayed U.S. labour data, a short trading week around Thanksgiving, and visible stress in parts of the crypto and equity complex. For finance students, it is a useful snapshot of how markets digest fresh macro information when liquidity is lower than usual.

In broad terms, the week can be framed around four themes:

  • labour market slack continues to build,
  • the Fed’s message stays cautious and somewhat split,
  • equities hold up thanks to mega-cap tech, while breadth is weak,
  • Bitcoin and other risk assets outside mainstream equities come under pressure.

1. Macro Data: Labour Market Slack Is Now Clear in the Numbers

The most important macro development this week was the release of delayed U.S. labour market data, which confirmed something markets had already suspected: the labour market is loosening.

1.1 Payrolls and unemployment

  • Nonfarm payrolls showed an increase of around 119,000 jobs, a clear improvement compared with earlier weak months but still below the pace seen at the peak of the expansion.
  • The unemployment rate rose to about 4.4%, a four-year high. Importantly, the increase in unemployment was not only about job losses but also about more people coming back into the labour force and not finding work immediately.
  • Wage growth stayed moderate, at roughly 0.2% month-on-month and around the high-3% range year-on-year, consistent with cooling but not collapsing wage pressures.

For the macro narrative, this combination can be summarised as: “more jobs, but more unemployed workers”. From a policy perspective, it gives the Fed room to ease in the future, but it also raises concerns about the strength of household income and consumption.

1.2 Labour market slack indicators

Several indicators of labour market slack all moved in the same direction:

  • the unemployment rate reached a multi-year high,
  • survey measures of jobs being “hard to get” vs “plentiful” pointed toward rising difficulty in finding employment,
  • continuing claims rose, signalling that unemployed workers were taking longer to find new jobs.

For students, this is a clear example of how you should not look at only one labour indicator. Slack is a concept: you infer it from several measures moving in the same direction.

2. Monetary Policy: Fed Minutes and a Divided Outlook

With labour market slack now more visible, attention turned to what the Federal Reserve might do next. During the week, minutes and commentary from policymakers revealed that the Committee is divided on the pace and scale of future cuts.

2.1 Fed minutes: key points

  • Some officials argued that the rise in unemployment and growing slack justified further easing, potentially as soon as the December meeting.
  • Others remained focused on inflation risks, highlighting that underlying price pressures had not yet fully normalised.
  • Markets interpreted the minutes as confirmation of a data-dependent Fed rather than a pre-committed cutting cycle.

From a discount-rate perspective, investors still expect lower policy rates over the next 12–18 months, but the probability distribution shifted toward fewer or later cuts compared with earlier in the autumn.

2.2 Thanksgiving and liquidity

The week was also affected by the U.S. Thanksgiving holiday:

  • U.S. markets were closed on Thursday and traded half-day on Friday,
  • volumes were generally lower throughout the week,
  • macro news had a potentially larger impact on prices because there were fewer participants and less depth in order books.

This is important when you later read price charts: a large intraday move in a low-liquidity environment does not always mean a large shift in fundamentals.

3. Equities: Tech Strength vs Weak Breadth

Equity markets came into the week with cautious optimism. By the end of the period, the picture was mixed: headline indices held up relatively well, but the underlying breadth remained weak.

3.1 Headline performance

  • Major U.S. indices such as the S&P 500 and Nasdaq started the week with gains, helped by continued strength in mega-cap tech and AI-linked names.
  • Investors focused on the idea that more labour market slack would reduce inflation and eventually prompt further rate cuts, which is typically supportive for growth stocks.
  • Toward the end of the week, gains moderated as participants became more cautious and digested the implications of the jobs data.

3.2 Under the surface: breadth and dispersion

Despite stable or modestly higher indices, the damage under the surface remained clear:

  • a large share of individual stocks were well below their highs,
  • many mid-cap and smaller names had already experienced double-digit drawdowns,
  • sector performance was uneven, with technology and a few high-quality growth names doing much better than the median stock.

For anyone studying market structure, this is a reminder that a “bull market” can coexist with significant weakness across a large part of the equity universe. From a portfolio perspective, it raises questions about concentration risk and diversification.

4. Fixed Income & FX: Yields Ease, Dollar Dynamics

Bond and currency markets spent the week digesting the new labour-market information and the Fed minutes.

4.1 Bond yields

  • Short-term yields were sensitive to shifting expectations about the December meeting and early-2026 policy path.
  • Long-term yields moved lower at times, reflecting both the weaker labour backdrop and a modest reduction in term premium as the perceived probability of an uncontrolled inflation scenario declined.
  • Overall, the yield curve remained inverted, but with slightly lower yields across much of the curve compared to earlier in the quarter.

4.2 FX markets

In FX, the picture was more nuanced:

  • the U.S. dollar moved in a narrow range, balancing the impact of lower yields against its role as a safe-haven currency,
  • currencies of economies more exposed to global trade or to commodity cycles reacted to changes in risk appetite and rate differentials,
  • overall, FX moves were consistent with a world where growth worries are increasing but not yet dominant.

From a corporate finance angle, these FX moves matter for the translation of foreign earnings and for the competitiveness of exporters, particularly in sectors like technology, autos, and industrials.

5. Commodities: Stable Gold, Softer Oil

In commodities, the week did not bring dramatic new highs, but it did reinforce existing trends.

5.1 Precious metals

  • Gold remained elevated by historical standards, supported by a combination of lower yields, central-bank demand, and ongoing macro uncertainty.
  • Even without fresh record highs, the level of gold prices continued to signal strong demand for hedging and diversification.
  • Silver and other metals moved in line with a mix of safe-haven and industrial-demand drivers.

5.2 Energy and industrials

  • Oil prices eased somewhat as markets weighed softer demand indicators against ongoing supply constraints.
  • Industrial commodities reacted to revised expectations for global manufacturing and trade, especially in Europe and Asia.

For students, this is a good reminder that commodities often express the market’s view on both growth and risk: lower oil prices can signal concerns about future demand, while high gold prices signal concerns about uncertainty and financial risk.

6. Crypto and Alternatives: Bitcoin Sends a Caution Signal

One of the clearest warning signals this week came from crypto. Bitcoin and other major digital assets extended the decline that had started earlier in the month.

6.1 Bitcoin performance

  • Bitcoin traded more than 30% below its recent all-time high and recorded one of its worst months of 2025.
  • ETF outflows and losses among short-term holders contributed to selling pressure.
  • Analysts pointed to Bitcoin as an “early warning indicator” for risk appetite, especially for highly levered or speculative strategies.

For a student of markets, this behaviour is interesting because crypto often moves ahead of more traditional risk assets, especially when the story is about liquidity and speculative excess rather than pure macro fundamentals.

6.2 Other alternatives

Other alternative assets, such as private credit and real assets, did not show the same volatility on a weekly basis, but the broader conversation about valuations and liquidity risk continued in the background.

7. Global & Regional Stories

While U.S. data dominated the week, there were also important developments elsewhere:

  • in the UK, investors looked ahead to a key budget announcement, with gilts pricing the risk of higher taxes to stabilise public finances,
  • in the euro area, flash PMIs suggested modest growth and persistent divergence between stronger and weaker member states,
  • in some emerging markets, local bond auctions and currency moves reflected both domestic policy challenges and the global search for yield.

This reinforces a recurring point: even when the U.S. sets the tone for global markets, local factors can strongly influence relative performance across countries and regions.

8. What This Week Teaches a Finance Student

From a learning perspective, 24–30 November 2025 offers several lessons that are directly useful for both academic work and professional practice:

  • Slack vs growth: a labour market can be generating jobs and still be loosening if unemployment and continuing claims rise. When you model consumption or default risk, both aspects matter.
  • Fed communication: minutes and speeches are signals to be interpreted, not mechanical instructions. Markets responded to the distribution of views inside the Fed, not just to the last decision.
  • Headline indices vs breadth: when a few mega-cap names hold up the index while many stocks trade far below their highs, the risk profile of “the market” is very different from what the index level suggests.
  • Liquidity and seasonality: short holiday weeks can amplify price moves without a proportional change in fundamentals. When you backtest strategies or interpret charts, controlling for this is important.
  • Alternative signals: crypto, gold, and other non-traditional assets can act as early warning indicators for shifts in risk appetite and liquidity conditions.

In summary, the week of 24–30 November 2025 marked a shift from political drama to data-driven concern about growth and labour markets, with mixed but telling signals across equities, bonds, FX, commodities, and crypto. For anyone studying or practising finance, it’s a neat case study in how markets react when macro data finally catch up with what sentiment had already started to price in.

Key takeaways (24–30 November 2025):
  • Labour market slack becomes visible: unemployment and continuing claims rise.
  • Fed minutes reveal a split committee and a data-dependent stance.
  • Headline indices hold up, but breadth remains weak and uneven.
  • Gold stays elevated; oil softens on growth concerns.
  • Bitcoin slumps, acting as an early warning signal on risk appetite.
