Weekly Market Recap — 1–7 December 2025

Published: Dec 8, 2025 • Reading time: ~10 min

This fifth weekly recap covers the period from 1 to 7 December 2025. The key message is that U.S. consumers kept spending aggressively at the start of the holiday season, even as confidence indicators dropped sharply. At the same time, equity markets remained relatively stable, reinforcing the idea that investors still see a potential soft landing rather than an imminent recession.

For finance students, this week is a clear example of how behaviour (spending) and sentiment (confidence) can diverge, and what that means for earnings, valuations, and macro assumptions in models.

  • holiday sales and retail indicators were surprisingly strong,
  • consumer confidence dropped, highlighting a “mood vs behaviour” gap,
  • equities stayed close to recent highs, with contained volatility,
  • rate expectations and yields adjusted slightly but without a regime change.

1. Macro Picture: Consumers Spend, Confidence Falls

The macro narrative this week revolved around the beginning of the holiday shopping season and what it tells us about the strength of the U.S. consumer.

1.1 Retail sales and holiday season

  • Retail sales rose around 0.2% month-on-month and by more than 4% year-on-year, signalling that households are still willing to spend despite tighter financial conditions.
  • Early numbers from the Thanksgiving–Cyber Monday period showed record participation from shoppers, with both in-store and online channels performing strongly.
  • Projections for total U.S. holiday sales pointed to a new high in nominal terms, supported by a combination of volume and still-elevated prices.

From a corporate finance angle, this is positive for revenue projections in sectors like discretionary retail, e-commerce, logistics, and payments. It also supports short-term earnings resilience for consumer-facing businesses.

1.2 Consumer confidence

Paradoxically, while spending activity was strong, survey measures of consumer confidence fell noticeably.

  • Confidence indices dropped well below their long-term averages.
  • Households reported concern about inflation, income prospects, and the broader economic outlook.
  • The “expectations” component (how people see the next 6–12 months) was particularly weak.

This disconnect between what consumers say and what they do is crucial for modelling. In the short run, spending can remain strong even when people feel pessimistic, especially if they have accumulated savings or access to credit. Over a longer horizon, however, sustained low confidence often leads to slower consumption growth.

2. Monetary Policy & Inflation Expectations

Although there was no Federal Reserve meeting this week, markets continued to refine expectations for the December decision and for 2026.

2.1 Policy expectations

  • Investors still expected at least one more rate cut within the next few meetings, but the probability assigned to an immediate December move remained subject to incoming data.
  • Disinflation progress earlier in the year supported the narrative that the policy stance is gradually shifting from restrictive toward neutral.
  • However, stronger-than-expected consumer spending kept alive the possibility that inflation might prove stickier than headline numbers suggest.

For valuation, the key point is that discount rate assumptions are not moving dramatically week to week, but small shifts in the expected path of policy still influence growth vs value, long vs short duration, and cross-asset preferences.

3. Equities: Stability Near Highs, Rotation Underneath

Equity indices were relatively calm, especially compared with earlier weeks marked by political risk and labour-market surprises.

3.1 Index-level performance

  • Major U.S. indices held near recent highs, with small weekly gains or flat performance depending on the benchmark.
  • There were no extreme moves similar to previous weeks’ shocks (e.g. shutdown, crypto slump), which helped volatility stay contained.
  • Global indices reflected a similar pattern: modest moves at the aggregate level, with more action at sector level.

3.2 Sector behaviour

Beneath the stable surface, there were still interesting rotations:

  • Consumer-related sectors (retail, e-commerce, payments) benefited from strong holiday sales data and upbeat revenue expectations.
  • Defensive sectors remained supported by investors who do not fully trust the soft-landing narrative and still want earnings stability.
  • Rate-sensitive growth names in tech and communication services moved mostly in line with small changes in yields and expectations about future policy.

For students, the key point is that equity markets were not in a risk-off mode, but neither were they euphoric. The balance between cyclical exposure and defensives suggests a “wait-and-see” stance, consistent with mixed macro signals.

4. Fixed Income: Yields, Curves, and the Message from Bonds

In fixed income, the week’s message can be summarised as modest adjustment rather than regime change.

4.1 Yield movements

  • Short-term yields drifted in line with shifting probabilities of a December cut, but without large breaks.
  • Long-term yields remained anchored by disinflation expectations and by the view that real growth is slowing but still positive.
  • The yield curve remained inverted, but less extremely than at the peak of the tightening cycle earlier in the year.

This pattern is consistent with markets pricing a controlled transition from restrictive to more neutral monetary policy, not a sudden pivot to emergency easing.

4.2 Credit spreads

  • Investment grade spreads remained tight, reflecting solid corporate balance sheets and strong demand for relatively safe yield.
  • High yield spreads were stable to slightly wider, reflecting caution about weaker credits in a slower-growth environment.

In modelling cost of capital (WACC) or credit risk, this week reinforces the idea that credit conditions are not benign but also not stressed. Funding is available, but investors are selective and sensitive to leverage and cash-flow visibility.

5. FX & Cross-Border Dynamics

Foreign exchange markets responded to a combination of changes in risk appetite and relative growth expectations.

  • The U.S. dollar traded in a tight range overall, balancing lower longer-term yields with still-attractive carry relative to some peers.
  • Currencies of economies with stronger growth momentum or higher real yields attracted flows from investors seeking diversification.
  • For export-oriented markets, exchange-rate moves continued to be a key driver of equity performance.

For finance students, FX is a reminder that any DCF or valuation of multinational firms must be consistent with assumptions about currency levels and volatility.

6. Commodities & Alternatives: Demand Signals and Hedging

6.1 Commodities

  • Oil prices reflected a tug of war between strong near-term demand (holiday travel, logistics) and concerns about softer global growth in 2026.
  • Gold remained elevated as a hedge against both macro and policy uncertainty, though without the explosive moves seen earlier in the year.
  • Industrial metals continued to price expectations for global manufacturing and Chinese demand.

6.2 Cryptoassets

Crypto markets showed more muted price action compared with the sharp declines of late November. Volatility remained high in relative terms, but the week was more about consolidation than about a new leg lower or higher.

From a cross-asset perspective, this suggested that the immediate shock phase had passed, even if broader risk appetite was still fragile in more speculative corners of the market.

7. Interpreting the Week: What It Means for Models and Cases

Putting together macro, markets, and sentiment, the week of 1–7 December 2025 supports a version of the soft-landing story, but with important caveats. For finance students, several points are especially useful:

  • Behaviour vs sentiment: when consumers keep spending despite weak confidence, short-term earnings may look fine, but you should be cautious in extrapolating current consumption growth too far into the future.
  • Top-down consistency: your revenue assumptions for retail, travel, and services should be consistent with both macro data (sales, income) and with what you assume about future rates and inflation.
  • Cross-asset checks: if equity markets price a soft landing, but credit spreads or yields tell a different story, you need to decide which signal you trust more in your analysis.
  • Short vs long horizon: in the short term, markets can be fully supported by strong holiday spending. Over a multi-year horizon, the sustainability of consumption depends on real income, employment, and debt dynamics.
  • Volatility regime: the relatively calm behaviour of major indices this week does not eliminate prior risks; it just suggests that, for now, markets are comfortable with the balance between growth and inflation.

In summary, 1–7 December 2025 shows a market that is cautiously optimistic: macro data are good enough to support earnings, inflation is not re-accelerating, and policy is gradually shifting in a more supportive direction. At the same time, the gap between what people feel and what they do reminds us that the current equilibrium is not guaranteed to last.

Key takeaways (1–7 December 2025):
  • Holiday sales are strong, confirming short-term resilience of the U.S. consumer.
  • Consumer confidence drops, highlighting a growing mood–behaviour divergence.
  • Equities remain near highs, consistent with a soft-landing narrative.
  • Yields move modestly; policy expectations evolve but do not shift regime.
  • Cross-asset signals point to cautious, not euphoric, risk appetite.
